RAG — Legal 500 Regulatory / Public & Administrative Law
1. Purpose
This RAG helps RankPilot evaluate, classify and improve Legal 500 submissions involving:
Public Law
Administrative Law
Public Procurement
Planning
Environment
Local Government
Government Affairs
Parliamentary/Public Affairs
Regulatory work
Constitutional matters
Judicial review
Sector-specific regulatory practices
The system must understand that Legal 500 evaluates regulatory work through a combination of:
legal sophistication;
market visibility;
strength of team;
client roster;
consistency of practice;
commercial impact;
strategic positioning.
2. Foundational Principle
Legal 500 does not treat “regulatory” as one unified category.
Regulatory work is distributed across:
standalone public law sections;
procurement;
planning/environment;
sector-specific practices;
government relations;
regulated industries.
RankPilot must therefore determine:
the regulatory centre of gravity;
the correct Legal 500 category;
whether the matter demonstrates market-leading capability.
3. Core Classification Rule
Before assigning a matter to Public Law or Administrative Law, RankPilot must ask:
Is the regulatory issue fundamentally sectoral, transactional, constitutional, procurement-related or governmental?
If yes, the system must route the matter to the more appropriate Legal 500 category.
Examples:
telecom regulation → TMT/Telecoms;
energy permits → Energy;
environmental licensing → Environment;
health regulator issue → Healthcare/Life Sciences;
financial regulator issue → Banking/Financial Services;
sanctions/export controls → International Trade;
procurement challenge → Public Procurement;
lobbying/public affairs → Parliamentary & Public Affairs.
4. Legal 500 — What Researchers Usually Care About
Legal 500 researchers typically care about:
sophistication of advice;
visibility in major public/regulatory matters;
consistency of mandates;
reputation among peers and clients;
ability to handle contentious + advisory work;
relationship with regulators and public authorities;
strategic role in sensitive matters;
cross-disciplinary capability;
commercial relevance of the work;
depth and cohesion of the team.
Unlike Chambers, Legal 500 often places greater weight on:
breadth of practice;
client service;
team structure;
market consistency;
visible institutional positioning.
5. Public Law / Administrative Law — Positive Indicators
A matter belongs here when it substantially involves:
judicial review;
constitutional litigation;
administrative litigation;
legality of government action;
powers of public authorities;
administrative appeals;
public authority investigations;
statutory interpretation involving government powers;
constitutional rights;
public sector governance;
public policy implementation;
disciplinary proceedings;
state liability;
public authority decision-making.
6. Public Procurement — Positive Indicators
Use Public Procurement when the core issue involves:
government acquisition processes;
public tenders;
procurement structuring;
procurement litigation;
bid challenges;
tender strategy;
framework agreements;
procurement compliance;
public purchasing rules.
Legal 500 often treats procurement as:
highly technical;
process-sensitive;
strategically important for infrastructure and public services.
7. Planning / Environment
Planning and Environment should be treated separately when:
zoning dominates;
planning permission dominates;
land use dominates;
environmental compliance dominates;
environmental permitting dominates;
environmental litigation dominates.
If the issue instead concerns:
constitutional legality;
government powers;
procurement;
concessions;
administrative authority;
 then Public Law may still be more appropriate.
8. Sector-Specific Regulatory Rule
If a regulator is tied to a specific industry, RankPilot must prioritise the sector practice.
Examples:
telecom authority → Telecoms/TMT;
energy regulator → Energy;
financial regulator → Banking/Financial Services;
health regulator → Healthcare/Life Sciences;
aviation authority → Aviation;
environmental agency → Environment;
competition authority → Competition;
labour inspectorate → Employment;
tax authority → Tax.
9. Sophistication Indicators
A strong Legal 500 regulatory matter usually includes:
high-profile public issue;
economically significant project;
constitutional dimension;
politically sensitive issue;
strategic government interaction;
novel legal issue;
public impact;
multi-agency complexity;
major infrastructure or concession element;
cross-border regulatory coordination;
litigation + advisory overlap;
successful regulatory approval;
strategic procurement challenge;
major compliance transformation;
market-changing implications.
10. Team Assessment Logic
Legal 500 heavily evaluates the practice as a team, not only isolated matters.
RankPilot should therefore assess:
depth of partner bench;
consistency of mandates;
visible leadership;
specialist sub-teams;
cross-practice integration;
institutional clients;
recurring instructions;
public visibility;
market reputation.
11. Submission Weaknesses
RankPilot should flag:
matters described as generic “regulatory advice”;
missing regulator names;
missing explanation of government interaction;
lack of public impact;
lack of constitutional/administrative complexity;
routine compliance framed as flagship work;
unclear role of the firm;
absence of strategic narrative;
no evidence of team depth;
no institutional positioning.
12. Legal 500 Narrative Guidance
A strong submission should explain:
what authority/regulator was involved;
what was at stake;
why the issue mattered commercially or publicly;
what made the issue difficult;
what strategy the team designed;
why the client selected the firm;
what the outcome was;
why this demonstrates market leadership.
13. Legal 500 — Output Structure
For each matter, RankPilot should return:
A. Suggested category
Primary category
 Alternative category
 Rejected categories
B. Why this fits Legal 500
C. Public/Regulatory sophistication level
D. Team-strength relevance
E. Submission weaknesses
F. Rewrite recommendations

RAG — Leaders League Public Law / Regulatory / Government Affairs
1. Purpose
This RAG helps RankPilot classify and optimise Leaders League submissions involving:
Public Law
Government Affairs
Regulatory practices
Procurement
Infrastructure concessions
Environment
Public sector regulation
Government-facing strategy
2. Foundational Principle
Leaders League is more commercially and market-positioning driven than Chambers.
The system must therefore evaluate:
market authority;
visible positioning;
client prestige;
business relevance;
recognised expertise;
differentiation versus competitors.
3. Core Rule
Leaders League rewards firms that are visibly recognised by the market as:
go-to advisers;
institutional references;
sector specialists;
strategic advisers.
Classification alone is insufficient.
The system must determine whether the submission demonstrates:
recognisable market authority in a defined regulatory vertical.
4. Classification Logic
Public Law
Use when:
constitutional matters;
government powers;
administrative litigation;
judicial review;
concessions;
PPPs;
public authority interaction;
procurement;
administrative strategy.
Government Affairs / Public Affairs
Use when:
lobbying;
policy strategy;
legislative advocacy;
regulatory change;
stakeholder management;
political interface.
Sector Regulatory
Route to vertical categories whenever:
industry expertise dominates;
the regulator is sector-specific;
the practice is commercially specialised.
5. Leaders League — What Matters Most
The system should evaluate:
market reputation;
recognisable clients;
recurring mandates;
institutional visibility;
business sophistication;
strategic impact;
influence in regulated sectors;
prominence of lawyers;
visibility of leadership.
6. Sophistication Indicators
Strong Leaders League matters usually involve:
major concessions;
infrastructure projects;
regulatory crises;
politically sensitive issues;
strategic government negotiations;
public policy impact;
sector dominance;
national visibility;
large institutional clients;
landmark procurement work.
7. Weakness Indicators
Flag when:
matters are overly academic;
no commercial impact is shown;
the client significance is unclear;
no market positioning emerges;
no differentiation from competitors exists;
the work appears routine.
8. Output Structure
A. Suggested Leaders League category
B. Market positioning assessment
C. Regulatory vertical identified
D. Competitive strength level
E. Market differentiation gaps
F. Recommended narrative angle

RAG — IFLR Regulatory Overlay
1. Purpose
This RAG helps RankPilot identify when regulatory issues are relevant within IFLR1000 submissions.
IFLR is not a general Public Law ranking guide.
Regulatory work should only be evaluated when connected to:
finance;
banking;
capital markets;
investment funds;
restructuring;
infrastructure;
project finance;
energy;
transactions.
2. Foundational Principle
IFLR treats regulatory issues as:
transactional and financial overlays.
The system must therefore ask:
Does the regulatory issue materially affect the financing, transaction, project or financial structure?
If not, the matter likely does not belong in IFLR.
3. Positive Indicators
Relevant IFLR regulatory matters include:
banking regulation;
licensing of financial institutions;
fintech regulation;
capital markets approvals;
securities regulation;
financial regulator investigations;
insurance regulation;
investment fund regulation;
financial sector M&A approvals;
infrastructure concessions tied to financing;
energy regulatory approvals tied to projects;
PPP financing;
regulatory aspects of structured finance;
sanctions/export control affecting transactions.
4. Negative Rule
Do not treat the following as core IFLR matters unless materially tied to finance or transactions:
generic constitutional litigation;
ordinary public law disputes;
judicial review unrelated to transactions;
general procurement;
routine compliance;
public affairs/lobbying;
electoral matters;
purely administrative disputes.
5. IFLR Sophistication Indicators
High-value IFLR regulatory matters usually involve:
financial complexity;
transactional risk;
cross-border financing;
project finance;
infrastructure investment;
capital markets implications;
regulator approvals essential to closing;
complex licensing structures;
innovative financial products;
systemic financial relevance.
6. Team Evaluation
RankPilot should evaluate:
integration between regulatory and transactional teams;
cross-border capability;
sophistication of financial structuring;
relationship with regulators;
prominence in the financial market.
7. Submission Weaknesses
Flag when:
the regulatory issue is disconnected from finance;
no transactional relevance exists;
the regulator is mentioned superficially;
the financing structure is unclear;
no deal impact is shown;
the matter looks like ordinary administrative work.
8. Output Structure
A. Relevant IFLR category
B. Transactional relevance assessment
C. Regulatory overlay identified
D. Financial sophistication level
E. Deal-criticality analysis
F. Narrative optimisation recommendations
