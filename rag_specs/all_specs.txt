Global Lawyer Leadership Framework — RankPilot RAG v1
Purpose
This framework introduces a transversal human-capital and leadership layer applicable to all RankPilot practice-area RAGs.
The objective is to ensure that RankPilot does not evaluate only:
transactions,
matters,
structures,
complexity,
categories.
It must also evaluate:
lawyer visibility,
institutional trust,
client relationships,
negotiation leadership,
succession depth,
market perception,
team structure,
reputation continuity.
This layer should apply across:
Banking & Finance
Corporate/M&A
Disputes
Tax
Fintech
Projects
Arbitration
Employment
Capital Markets
Restructuring
all future practice-area RAGs.

1. Core principle
Legal rankings are not purely transaction-based systems.
They are systems of:
trust,
reputation,
visibility,
relationship continuity,
perceived leadership,
market influence.
A technically strong matter may still underperform if:
lawyer visibility is weak,
the practice appears overdependent on one partner,
succession is unclear,
relationship ownership is invisible,
leadership roles are not surfaced.
RankPilot must therefore evaluate both:
Matter strength
and
Human leadership strength
simultaneously.

2. Lawyer leadership dimensions
A. Relationship Leadership
Questions RankPilot should ask:
Who owns the client relationship?
Is the relationship institutional or individual?
Is the client recurring?
Is the lawyer trusted by institutional clients?
Does the matter reinforce long-term client continuity?
Strong indicators:
repeat mandates
long-term lender/sponsor/client relationships
recurring institutional engagements
repeat cross-border coordination
visible relationship partner
Weak indicators:
one-off engagement
unclear relationship ownership
no continuity narrative

B. Negotiation Leadership
Questions:
Who led negotiations?
Who coordinated counterparties?
Who managed execution pressure?
Who solved strategic issues?
Strong indicators:
lead negotiator
coordinating counsel
strategic deal architect
partner-led negotiations
multi-party coordination
Weak indicators:
generic “firm advised” wording
no visible leadership
no distinction between execution and strategy

C. Technical Leadership
Questions:
Which lawyers handled the most complex aspects?
Was specialist expertise visible?
Did the team solve technically difficult issues?
Strong indicators:
specialist structuring role
governance leadership
financing architecture
regulatory strategy
litigation strategy
tax structuring leadership
Weak indicators:
no identified specialist expertise
generic matter description

D. Market Visibility
Questions:
Are lawyers recognised in the market?
Are they visible beyond the submission?
Are they perceived as market leaders?
Strong indicators:
market-facing partners
visible leadership positions
highly recognised practitioners
recurring presence in sophisticated mandates
visible strategic clients
Weak indicators:
invisible leadership team
no identified stars
weak differentiation from competitors

E. Succession Depth
Questions:
Does the practice depend on one rainmaker?
Is the next generation visible?
Are senior associates or junior partners surfaced?
Strong indicators:
visible next-generation partners
succession planning
multiple client-facing lawyers
distributed leadership
team continuity
Weak indicators:
founder dependency
single-partner concentration
no visible bench strength
invisible senior associates

F. Institutionalization
Questions:
Is the practice institutionalized?
Does the client trust the platform or only the individual?
Is there integrated team execution?
Strong indicators:
multi-partner involvement
coordinated teams
integrated cross-practice work
repeat institutional mandates
Weak indicators:
isolated rainmaker model
fragmented execution
no evidence of coordinated team structure

Matter Reconstruction Assistant
1. Problema identificado
Las firmas no tienen el problema solo al “redactar” submissions. El problema empieza antes: no tienen una forma eficiente, estructurada y estratégica de reconstruir los matters ocurridos 8-12 meses atrás.
La información existe, pero está dispersa en:
cadenas de correos,
documentos adjuntos,
closing emails,
versiones de contratos,
minutas,
facturación,
memoria de socios/asociados.
El resultado: el equipo prepara submissions con información incompleta, recordada tarde, sin contexto y sin jerarquización estratégica.
2. Objetivo de esta capa
El Matter Reconstruction Assistant no busca escribir el submission final.
Su objetivo es anterior:
convertir evidencia documental dispersa en una ficha inteligente del matter.
Debe responder:
¿Qué ocurrió?
¿Quién participó?
¿Cuál era el problema del cliente?
¿Qué hizo la firma?
¿Cuál fue la complejidad real?
¿Qué resultado se obtuvo?
¿Qué señales reputacionales tiene?
¿Por qué podría importar para Chambers / Legal 500 / rankings?
El output ideal sería una Matter Intelligence Card.
Ejemplo:
Matter: Financiamiento transfronterizo para X cliente

Resumen:
La firma asesoró a [cliente] en [tipo de operación], involucrando [jurisdicciones], [partes] y [estructura].

2. Regla crítica de clasificación
No todo litigio entra en Dispute Resolution general.
El RAG debe aplicar esta regla:
Si existe una categoría específica para el tipo de disputa, el asunto debe ir allí antes que a Litigation / Dispute Resolution general.
Ejemplos:

Tax dispute → Tax Litigation
Labour dispute → Labour & Employment
Competition damages/cartel → Competition Litigation
Real estate possession/lease/development dispute → Real Estate Litigation
Financial crime/criminal investigation → Financial Crime / White-Collar Crime
Civil fraud/freezing orders/asset tracing → Fraud: Civil
Arbitration internacional → International Arbitration / Arbitration
Claimant mass action → Group Litigation / Mass Torts / Class Actions. 
Si no existe una categoría específica, el sistema debe hacer esto:
Regla complementaria
Si no existe una categoría más específica en esa guía / jurisdicción, el matter puede entrar en Litigation / Dispute Resolution general, siempre que sea una disputa relevante, sofisticada y suficientemente conectada con el core de esa categoría.
Pero con una condición importante:
El sistema debe dejar constancia de que revisó la posibilidad de una categoría específica y no encontró una mejor opción.
Es decir, no debe asumir “general disputes” por default. Debe llegar ahí por descarte razonado.
Lógica RAG
IF specific category exists
→ route matter to specific category

ELSE IF dispute is commercial / civil / corporate / arbitration / white-collar within the accepted scope of the jurisdiction
→ allow in Dispute Resolution / Litigation general

ELSE
→ flag as low-fit or out-of-scope
Ejemplo
País A no tiene Tax Litigation

País B sí tiene Tax Litigation
Ese mismo matter debería decir:
Category fit: Weak for Dispute Resolution general.
Better category: Tax Litigation.
Risk: Submission dilution / misclassification.
Entonces la regla completa sería
Primero buscar categoría específica.
 Si existe, priorizarla.
 Si no existe, evaluar si el asunto cae razonablemente dentro del paraguas general de Litigation / Dispute Resolution.
 Si cae, aceptarlo con nota de weighting risk.
 Si no cae, excluirlo.
Esto es importantísimo porque evita que RankPilot sea rígido. No puede decir “Tax nunca entra en Disputes”. Debe decir: “Tax entra en Disputes solo si Chambers no ofrece una vía más específica y si el mercado lo trata como parte del disputes practice.”

RAG Regulatory / Public & Administrative Law
1. Purpose
This RAG helps RankPilot evaluate, classify and improve submissions involving regulatory, administrative, public law, government-facing, constitutional, procurement, concessions, public affairs, licensing, enforcement and sector-specific regulatory work.
Its core purpose is to determine whether a matter belongs in:
Public Law / Administrative & Public Law
Public Procurement
Government / Government Relations
Political / Electoral Law
Public International Law
Environmental Regulatory
Sector-specific regulatory categories
Or another more precise practice area such as Energy, Life Sciences, TMT, Data Protection, Competition, Labour, Tax, Healthcare, Infrastructure, Planning, Urban Law, etc.
2. Foundational Principle
Regulatory work is not a single practice area.
It is a cross-practice dimension that appears across many rankings, sectors and jurisdictions.
RankPilot must never classify a matter as Public Law / Administrative Law merely because a public authority, licence, regulator, sanction, tender, permit or statute is involved.
The system must first identify the regulatory centre of gravity.
3. Mandatory Classification Rule
Before assigning a matter to Public Law / Administrative & Public Law, RankPilot must ask:
Is there a more specific ranking category that better captures the matter’s sector, regulator, legal issue or commercial context?
If yes, the matter should be routed to that more specific category.
Public Law / Administrative Law should function as a sophisticated residual category, not as a generic container for anything involving the State.
4. Regulatory Centre of Gravity Test
For each matter, RankPilot must identify the dominant regulatory axis:
A. Government power / administrative action
Use Public Law / Administrative Law when the issue concerns:
powers of central or local government;
administrative acts;
judicial review;
statutory appeals;
constitutional challenges;
government decision-making;
administrative proceedings;
public authority duties;
state liability;
public policy implementation;
disciplinary proceedings;
interaction between private parties and the State.
B. Public procurement / tenders
Use Public Procurement when the matter concerns:
acquisition of goods or services by public entities;
tender design;
tender participation;
procurement challenges;
bid protests;
award disputes;
procurement compliance;
public buyer/supplier relationships.
If the issue is not simply procurement but a broader concession, infrastructure project, PPP or privatization, consider Public Law, Projects, Infrastructure, Energy, Transport or sector-specific categories.
C. Concessions / PPPs / privatizations
Use Public Law, Projects or Infrastructure depending on the guide and country when the matter concerns:
public-private partnerships;
concessions;
privatizations;
regulated public services;
long-term operation of public assets;
concession bidding;
concession amendments;
disputes with granting authorities;
regulatory economics of public infrastructure.
D. Sector-specific regulation
Route to the relevant sector-specific practice when the core issue is tied to a specific regulated industry.
Examples:
Energy regulator / power generation / transmission / renewables → Energy & Natural Resources.
Telecom regulator / spectrum / connectivity / telecom licensing → Telecommunications.
Healthcare regulator / hospitals / health insurance / medical services → Healthcare Regulatory.
Life sciences / pharma / medical devices / ANVISA-style issues → Life Sciences.
Data privacy authority / GDPR / LGPD / data processing → Data Protection.
Environmental licensing / pollution / waste / water / emissions → Environment.
Food labelling / additives / pesticides / consumer safety → Regulatory: Agro/Food.
Gambling licences / betting rules → Gambling & Betting.
Aviation authority / airport slots / safety rules → Aviation.
Urban zoning / land use / planning permissions → Planning / Urban Law.
Financial services regulator → Banking & Finance / Financial Services Regulation.
Antitrust authority / merger control / cartel / dominance → Competition.
Tax authority / assessments / tax litigation → Tax.
Labour inspectorate / employment enforcement → Labour & Employment.
5. Core Negative Rule
Do not classify a matter as Public Law merely because:
a regulator was involved;
a licence was required;
the client operates in a regulated industry;
the matter involved compliance;
a government agency was consulted;
public policy was indirectly relevant;
the matter required an administrative filing.
Those facts are indicators, not determinative.
RankPilot must classify based on the main legal and strategic issue.
6. Public Law / Administrative Law — Positive Indicators
A matter belongs in Public Law / Administrative Law when it substantially involves:
judicial review;
constitutional litigation;
administrative litigation;
statutory appeals;
public authority powers;
legality of government action;
human rights or civil liberties in relation to state action;
government decision-making;
public tenders, if no separate Public Procurement category is more appropriate;
concessions, PPPs or privatizations, if treated within Public Law in that jurisdiction;
regulatory advice not already captured by a specific practice area;
representation before constitutional courts or administrative tribunals;
relationship between individuals/private entities and government bodies.
7. Public Procurement — Positive Indicators
A matter belongs in Public Procurement when it substantially involves:
government acquisition of goods or services;
public tenders;
procurement structuring;
procurement disputes;
bid protests;
award challenges;
tender compliance;
public buyer/vendor advice;
procurement in education, healthcare, transport, housing, infrastructure or technology.
Related areas to check:
Projects;
Infrastructure;
Transport;
Healthcare;
Education;
IT;
Public Law;
Construction.
8. Government Relations / Public Affairs / Political Law
Use this branch when the matter involves:
lobbying;
legislative advocacy;
regulatory change;
public affairs strategy;
appropriations;
government relations;
policy influence;
advice to embassies, governments, NGOs or corporations seeking regulatory change;
political campaign rules;
campaign finance;
election advertising;
party registration;
voting rights;
election disputes;
candidate eligibility;
electoral litigation.
In Brazil, for example, Electoral Law should be treated separately from generic Public Law when the issue concerns campaign finance, political parties, electoral advertising, voting rights or proceedings before electoral courts.
9. Public International Law
Use Public International Law when the matter involves:
state-to-state disputes;
sovereign immunity;
sanctions;
boundary rights;
international humanitarian law;
international courts or tribunals;
disputes before the ICJ, ITLOS or ICC;
public international obligations of states.
Do not include investment arbitration here if the guide expressly places it under International Arbitration.
10. Environmental Regulatory
Use Environment or Regulatory: Environment when the matter involves:
environmental compliance;
environmental permitting;
pollution;
waste;
water;
wetlands;
endangered species;
emissions;
contaminated land;
environmental enforcement;
REACH/RoHS/WEEE-style regulation;
biocides;
chemicals;
single-use plastics;
environmental aspects of M&A or financing.
If the matter is about construction of energy plants, check Energy.
 If it is about planning permissions or zoning, check Planning / Urban Law.
 If it is about broader constitutional or administrative legality, check Public Law.
11. Country-Specific Sensitivity
RankPilot must not assume that one country’s category map applies globally.
For every analysis, it must first identify:
guide;
country;
exact category name;
neighbouring categories;
exclusions;
related sections;
regulator(s);
whether the guide treats the work as standalone or sector-specific.
Examples:
Brazil
Brazil requires high sensitivity because regulatory work is heavily fragmented across:
Public Law;
Electoral Law;
Environment;
Energy;
Telecommunications;
Data Protection;
Life Sciences;
Healthcare;
Gambling;
Urban Law;
Infrastructure;
Competition;
Tax;
Labour.
Public Law in Brazil may include procurement, privatizations, concessions and PPPs, but excludes regulatory work already captured by specific practice areas.
UK
UK Administrative & Public Law may include judicial review, statutory appeals, disciplinary proceedings, public authority powers, procurement, environmental issues and discrimination/employment issues.
But RankPilot must check related categories:
Civil Liberties & Human Rights;
Local Government;
Parliamentary & Public Affairs;
Public Inquiries;
Public Procurement;
Planning.
USA
USA Government may split into:
Government Contracts;
Government Relations;
Political Law.
The system must distinguish federal contracting/bid protests from lobbying and political law.
Europe
Europe may separate:
Public Law;
Public Procurement;
Public Law: Planning and Environment;
Regulatory: Agro/Food;
Regulatory: Environment;
Regulatory: Government & Public Affairs;
sector-specific regulatory practices.
Latin America
Latin America Public Law generally covers constitutional and administrative matters, public procurement and general regulatory advice, but excludes regulatory work related to specific practice areas such as Competition, Energy, Labour, Life Sciences, Tax, TMT and IP.
12. Matter Sophistication Scoring
RankPilot should score regulatory/public law matters higher when they include:
high economic value;
high public impact;
politically sensitive context;
strategic interaction with regulators;
novel legal issue;
lack of precedent;
constitutional dimension;
market-changing implications;
emergency or crisis context;
multi-agency coordination;
cross-border regulatory impact;
enforcement risk;
licence-critical issue;
concession/PPP complexity;
public procurement challenge;
litigation before constitutional or administrative courts;
reputational exposure;
involvement of senior lawyers;
successful outcome despite regulatory uncertainty.
13. Lower-Value Indicators
A matter may be less rankable if it is:
routine licence renewal;
standard regulatory filing;
generic compliance memo;
low-stakes administrative advice;
ordinary procurement support;
basic due diligence;
internal policy review;
regulatory monitoring without strategic impact;
isolated filing with no complexity;
descriptive rather than strategic.
However, RankPilot must not dismiss a matter only because it appears routine. It should ask whether the submission failed to explain:
what was at stake;
why the authority mattered;
what risk existed;
what made the strategy sophisticated;
what outcome was achieved.
14. Narrative Requirements for Strong Submissions
A strong Regulatory/Public Law matter should explain:
who the client was;
which authority/regulator was involved;
what decision, licence, sanction, tender, regulation or public act was at issue;
what was commercially or institutionally at stake;
why the issue was legally difficult;
whether there was regulatory uncertainty;
whether the matter had political/public sensitivity;
what strategy the firm designed;
what role senior lawyers played;
what result was achieved;
why this matter shows market-leading capability.
15. Diagnostic Questions
For each matter, RankPilot should ask:
What is the relevant jurisdiction?
What is the exact ranking category?
Is this public law general or sector-specific regulation?
Which regulator or public authority is central?
Is the client public, private, claimant, concessionaire, bidder, regulated company, NGO or political actor?
Is the issue contentious, advisory, transactional, licensing, lobbying, procurement or enforcement?
Is there a more specific category than Public Law?
Does the matter involve constitutional or administrative legality?
Does it involve procurement, concession, PPP or privatization?
Does it involve a regulated industry?
What was at stake?
What made the work complex?
What outcome was achieved?
Which lawyers led the strategy?
Why would a ranking researcher care?
16. Red Flags in Submissions
RankPilot should flag when:
the country is missing;
the category is unclear;
the regulator is not identified;
the matter says “regulatory advice” without explaining the regulatory issue;
the submission does not clarify whether the work is Public Law or sector-specific;
procurement, concession and PPP work are mixed without distinction;
there is no outcome;
there is no explanation of strategic complexity;
the matter reads like routine compliance;
the public authority’s role is vague;
the client’s risk is unclear;
the firm’s role is described passively;
the matter lacks lawyer leadership;
the matter is potentially better suited to another practice area.
17. Output Format Required
For every matter, RankPilot should return:
A. Suggested classification
Primary category:
 Alternative category:
 Rejected categories:
B. Reasoning
Why this matter belongs in the suggested category.
C. Regulatory centre of gravity
Government power / procurement / sector regulation / enforcement / lobbying / constitutional / concessions / environmental / electoral / public international.
D. Sophistication level
Low / Medium / High / Exceptional.
E. Ranking strength
Weak / Usable / Strong / Flagship.
F. Narrative gaps
What is missing from the matter description.
G. Rewrite recommendation
How the firm should frame the matter for ranking purposes.
18. Example Logic
Example 1
Matter: Advice to a telecom company on spectrum licence renewal before the national telecom regulator.
Classification: Telecommunications.
 Not Public Law.
 Reason: sector-specific regulator and licence issue dominate.
Example 2
Matter: Judicial review of a government decision cancelling a concession.
Classification: Public Law / Administrative Law or Projects/Infrastructure, depending on guide.
 Reason: administrative legality + concession/public asset issue.
Example 3
Matter: Challenge to award of a hospital services tender.
Classification: Public Procurement.
 Alternative: Healthcare, if the guide treats healthcare procurement separately.
 Reason: tender award challenge dominates.
Example 4
Matter: Advice to food company on EU labelling, additives and packaging rules.
Classification: Regulatory: Agro/Food.
 Reason: sector-specific food regulation.
Example 5
Matter: Representation of political party in campaign finance dispute before electoral court.
Classification: Electoral Law / Political Law.
 Not Public Law general.
 Reason: electoral system and campaign finance dominate.
Example 6
Matter: Constitutional challenge to a new tax imposed by government.
Classification: Tax Litigation or Constitutional/Public Law depending on guide.
 Decision rule: if tax liability is the commercial centre, use Tax. If legality of state power is the centre, consider Public Law.
19. Core Instruction for the Model
RankPilot must behave like a senior rankings consultant.
It should not merely detect keywords such as “regulator,” “government,” “licence,” “public authority” or “compliance.”
It must classify the matter by asking:
What is the legal, commercial and institutional centre of gravity of this work?
Then it must determine whether the matter is best positioned as:
Public Law;
Administrative Law;
Public Procurement;
Government Relations;
Political / Electoral Law;
Public International Law;
Environment;
or a sector-specific regulatory category.
20. Golden Rule
Public Law is not the default home for regulatory work.
It is the right category only when the matter’s real value lies in the relationship between the client and the State, the legality of government action, public authority powers, administrative/constitutional proceedings, procurement, concessions, PPPs or non-sector-specific regulatory advice.
For everything else, RankPilot must search for the more precise regulatory home.

CRITERIO BASE PARA CUALQUIER RANKING INDIVIDUAL

El sistema debe evaluar a cada abogado/a bajo cinco dimensiones:
A. Evidencia transaccional o técnica
El abogado debe estar asociado a matters concretos y relevantes. No basta decir que “tiene experiencia”. El sistema debe detectar:
en cuántos matters aparece;
qué rol tuvo;
si lideró, estructuró, negoció, coordinó o solo participó;
si los asuntos son sofisticados;
si los clientes son relevantes;
si hay evidencia de complejidad técnica;
si el trabajo corresponde realmente al área de práctica.
Para RankPilot, esta es la capa más importante.
B. Rol real dentro del matter
No todos los abogados que aparecen en un asunto tienen el mismo peso.
El sistema debe distinguir entre:
Lead partner: máxima relevancia.
Co-lead / strategic partner: muy relevante.
Key supporting partner: relevante si hay evidencia concreta.
Senior associate / counsel con rol sustantivo: potencial para Associate to Watch, Rising Star o Next Generation.
Nombre listado sin rol claro: baja evidencia.
La descripción del matter debe reforzar la candidatura individual.
Ejemplo:
“Fernando Eraña led the structuring of the collateral package…”
es mucho más útil que:
“The team advised…”
Porque además del ranking individual, esto fortalece el bench strength institucional de la práctica.
C. Reputación y validación externa
El sistema debe valorar si el abogado tiene:
ranking actual;
historial de rankings;
menciones en directorios;
premios;
feedback de clientes;
reconocimiento de pares;
publicaciones o visibilidad técnica;
liderazgo institucional.
Pero esto no debe sustituir la evidencia de trabajo. Debe complementarla.
D. Coherencia entre perfil y matters
El sistema debe verificar que el perfil del abogado dialogue con los asuntos.
Ejemplo:
Si Jorge Labastida se presenta como experto en structured finance, asset-backed lending y collateral packages, el sistema debe asignarle matters que prueben exactamente eso.
Si Fernando Eraña se presenta como fuerte en fintech, venture finance, secured lending o innovative debt structures, los matters seleccionados deben reforzar esa narrativa.
Si Emilio Carrillo aparece como banking/finance + regulatory + restructuring, debe tener asuntos que prueben esa combinación.
Si Eduardo Yrigoyen es candidato a Associate to Watch, el sistema debe mostrar que tuvo intervención sustantiva y recurrente, no meramente administrativa.
E. Seniority y categoría posible
El sistema debe sugerir una categoría realista:
Band ranking: socios con liderazgo probado, matters relevantes y reputación.
Up and Coming / Next Generation / Rising Star: abogados más jóvenes o en ascenso con evidencia fuerte.
Associate to Watch / Rising Star Associate: asociados sénior o counsel con participación visible en matters relevantes.
No candidate yet: si hay poca evidencia o si el rol no está claro.
